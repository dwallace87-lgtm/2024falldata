Enlightenment & Revolution – Assessment Data

Franklin County High School • 10th Grade World History • 2024–2025 School Year

Teacher: Donald Wallace Total students with complete data: 118 Performance task: "How did Reason lead to Revolution?"

Class level outcomes

Summary
Across five World History sections (118 students), class pre assessment averages clustered in the mid 50s. On the first argument essay, they rose into the low to mid 70s. Taken together, the overall average moved from about 55% on the pre assessment to about 72% on the essay. Growth by class ranged from roughly 16 to 18 points, which feels like real early-year progress, not perfection.

Pre assessment item snapshot

The table below summarizes the structure of the diagnostic pre assessment. Item level averages are not the main point here, but they help show how the baseline measure was built.

Item type Number of items Focus Scoring detail
Multiple choice 8 Core Enlightenment concepts, such as natural rights, social contract, consent of the governed, separation of powers, and basic ideas about the purpose of government. One point per correct answer. These items provided a quick read on prior knowledge and common misconceptions.
Matching 1 cluster Matching key thinkers (Locke, Montesquieu, Rousseau, Voltaire) to simplified descriptions of their ideas. Partial credit allowed for partially correct matches. Used primarily to see whether students recognized names and core ideas.
Short constructed response 1 Students responded to a simplified Locke style prompt: Why might Enlightenment writers argue that people have the right to challenge their government? Scored on a 0–3 scale for clarity, use of the text, and basic explanation. This writing sample informed early grouping and how much modeling I needed to do before the essay.

Note: Pre assessment items combined into a single percentage score for each student, which became the baseline for the growth data above.
