10th Grade World History • Franklin County High School • Inquiry Design Model

How did Reason lead to Revolution?

Inquiry Standard
Era7.2.WH.2 – Analyze the social, economic, and political ideas that influenced 18th- and 19th-century revolutions.
Staging the Compelling Question
Discuss the concept of inalienable rights using short excerpts from John Locke’s Two Treatises of Government, the Declaration of Independence, and the Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen. Use these texts to launch the compelling question: “How did Reason lead to Revolution?”

Unit Overview

This 13–15 day inquiry unit, based on the C3 “How did Reason lead to Revolution?” lesson and the Arkansas C3 model, guides my 10th graders through the Age of Enlightenment and its revolutionary impact. We begin with the compelling question “How did Reason lead to Revolution?” and scaffold supporting questions across the unit.

Each day follows a workshop model – mini-lesson, guided practice, independent work, and share/closure – to keep students actively engaged. I integrate accessible primary sources (for example, Locke, the Declaration of Independence, and the Rights of Man) and visuals from the C3 curriculum, adapting them for my diverse learners (including SPED and MLL students).

Frequent formative checks (notebook checks, quick-writes, evidence charts) let me monitor growth and make adjustments in real time. The unit culminates in a summative argumentative essay where students answer the compelling question with evidence from the unit. Throughout, we align to Kentucky Academic Standards, including: HS.WH.CE.4 (causes of revolutions), HS.WH.CH.6 (changing views of government and legitimacy), HS.WH.I.UC.3 and HS.WH.I.CC.2 (inquiry and argument skills), and HS.WH.KH.1 (connections to Kentucky history). My teacher voice is intentionally warm and encouraging – I use “we” and “our class” language and constantly explain why each activity helps them answer the big question.

Daily Plans – Workshop Structure

Day 1 Introduction & Inalienable Rights (Staging the Question)
Day focus: Hook students with a visual of Enlightenment life, introduce inalienable rights, and pre-assess what they already believe about rights and government.
Mini Lesson
  • Project an image of an 18th-century salon (Madame Geoffrin’s salon, 1755) and ask, “What might people in this scene be discussing – rights, government, something else?”
  • Use student observations to frame the Age of Reason as a time when people debated rights and power.
  • Students complete a quick-write in their notebooks: “What rights do you think everyone is born with? Give examples.”
  • Introduce natural / inalienable rights with examples (life, liberty, property, free speech, education).
  • Explicitly reference HS.WH.CE.4 (revolutions) and HS.WH.CH.6 (changing views of government), explaining that these rights will later challenge kings and empires.
  • Launch the compelling question: “How did Reason lead to Revolution?”
Guided Practice
  • In small groups, students read short, adapted excerpts from: Document 1 – Locke’s Two Treatises, Document 2 – Declaration of Independence, and Document 3 – Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen.
  • We do a think-aloud together on the board: underline phrases like “life, liberty, property,” “pursuit of happiness,” “men are born and remain free and equal in rights.”
  • MLL/ELL support: bilingual glossary for key terms (e.g. “inalienable,” “charter,” “liberté”) and permission to use translation apps.
  • SPED support: a partially completed Rights Chart organizer so students can record rights listed in each document without getting lost in the text.
Independent Practice
  • In notebooks, students respond to the prompt: “According to these documents, what are examples of inalienable rights?”
  • Students list at least three rights (for example, life, liberty, property, freedom of speech) and pose one question about rights (for example, “Is education an inalienable right?”).
  • Teacher quickly scans a few responses while students write, treating this as a baseline formative check.
Share / Closure
  • Invite a handful of students to read their lists aloud.
  • Build a class chart of inalienable rights on chart paper; leave it posted for reference all unit.
  • Make the Kentucky connection: “These Enlightenment ideas about rights show up in early Kentucky documents as well – for example, the 1792 Kentucky constitution guarantees freedom of religion.” Connect this explicitly to HS.WH.KH.1.
Materials / Resources
  • Projected image of an Enlightenment salon (Madame Geoffrin).
  • Adapted excerpts from Locke, Declaration of Independence, Rights of Man.
  • “Rights Chart” organizer (modified version for SPED/MLL).
  • Notebooks, chart paper, markers.
Day 2 SQ1 – What was the Enlightenment?
Day focus: Define the Enlightenment (Age of Reason) and clarify how Enlightenment writers thought about human rights.
Mini Lesson
  • Open with a short video clip or timeline that situates the Enlightenment between the Scientific Revolution and the age of revolutions.
  • On the board, write “Enlightenment = Age of Reason” and briefly unpack what “reason” meant in this context.
  • Preview key vocabulary: reason, rights, philosopher, progress. Students echo or sketch symbols for each word.
  • Connect to Kentucky’s inquiry focus (HS.WH.I.UC.1): explain that today they are “gathering information from sources to define a key concept.”
Guided Practice
  • Split the class into two reading groups: Group A reads a short History.com overview of the Enlightenment; Group B reads an adapted LiveScience article.
  • Each group highlights lines that answer:
    • “What is the Enlightenment?”
    • “What did it value?”
  • Groups meet in mixed pairs to jigsaw: A partners explain their article to B, and vice versa.
  • SPED support: provide reading guides with a simple frame: “The Enlightenment believed ___ because ___.” plus highlighters to keep track of main ideas.
Independent Practice
  • In notebooks, students write a 2–3 sentence definition: “The Enlightenment was a time when people used reason and science to explain government, society, and nature...”
  • They also list at least two thinkers from the readings (for example, Locke, Rousseau, Voltaire, Montesquieu).
  • Teacher circulates to confer and uses this as a quick formative checkpoint for SQ1 understanding.
Share / Closure
  • Volunteers read their definitions; teacher notes recurring words like reason, progress, equality, skepticism of authority.
  • Class collaborates on a one-sentence, “Our Working Definition of the Enlightenment.”
  • Preview Day 3: “Tomorrow we’ll build a chart of major Enlightenment thinkers so we can see who these people were and what they actually said about rights.”
Materials / Resources
  • Short video or slide timeline of the Enlightenment.
  • Adapted History.com and LiveScience articles.
  • Vocabulary list / visuals for key terms.
  • Notebooks, highlighters, reading guides.
Day 3 SQ1 – Chart of Enlightenment Thinkers
Day focus: Build a chart of major Enlightenment writers (who they were, where they lived, what they wrote, and what they believed about rights and society).
Mini Lesson
  • Quick warm-up: ask, “Why do we still care about Enlightenment writers?” and collect a few ideas.
  • Introduce the Thinkers Chart: columns for author, country, major work, big idea on rights/society.
  • Model a sample row for John Locke using yesterday’s text: fill in his name, England, one major work, and a short phrase like “people have natural rights and can challenge government.”
Guided Practice
  • Students work in groups of four. Each student is responsible for one writer (for example: Locke, Voltaire, Rousseau, Montesquieu).
  • Using short excerpts from GMU Human Rights documents, Biography.com “Famous People”, and the LiveScience overview, they pull out: where the person was from, one or two key works, and their central idea.
  • SPED/ELL scaffolds: groups receive partially filled charts with names and works already in place so they only have to complete “country” and “big idea” columns. Provide a small vocab box (for example, “sovereign = ruler; civil liberties = freedoms”).
Independent Practice
  • Each student completes their assigned row and then copies summary information from their group members so everyone has a full Thinkers Chart in their notebook.
  • If time runs short, students finish any missing pieces as homework.
  • Teacher does a quick notebook spot-check, leaving short comments like “Nice job naming ‘social contract’ for Rousseau” or “Check spelling of Montesquieu.”
Share / Closure
  • Post large chart papers around the room labeled with each thinker’s name. Students do a short Gallery Walk and jot one new fact from each.
  • Debrief with a few focus questions: “Who seems to have the most revolutionary idea about rights? Why?”
  • Exit ticket: students write a one-sentence definition of “natural rights” and “social contract” to confirm SQ1 vocabulary.
Materials / Resources
  • Thinkers Chart handout (student and poster versions).
  • Short bios/excerpts for Locke, Voltaire, Rousseau, Montesquieu.
  • Vocabulary support sheet.
  • Markers, chart paper, sticky notes.
Day 4 SQ2 – Problems Critiqued by Enlightenment (Part 1)
Day focus: Identify social, economic, and political problems that Enlightenment writers criticized in the Old Regime.
Mini Lesson
  • Briefly review the Old Regime: draw a pyramid of the Three Estates and label who falls where.
  • Preview vocabulary: monarchy, estate, inequality, censorship.
  • Ask students: “Looking at this system, what feels unfair or unstable?” and record their ideas (oppression, injustice, corruption).
Guided Practice
  • Students rotate through three stations: Station 1 – Locke (government & property), Station 2 – Montesquieu (tyranny & law), Station 3 – Adam Smith (economy & poverty).
  • At each station they answer: “What problem does this author point out?” and record it in an Issues Chart with columns: Social / Political / Economic.
  • Example patterns: Locke – taxation without consent (political); Smith – famine and unfair trade (economic); Montesquieu – abuse of absolute power (political/social).
  • SPED/ELL supports: simplified summaries at each station, a word bank of key terms, and a sentence frame such as “Montesquieu says that ______ is a problem because ______.”
Independent Practice
  • After rotating, each student adds at least one entry to each column of a large class Issues Chart posted on the wall.
  • Students complete an exit ticket: “Name one social problem and one political problem mentioned by Locke or Montesquieu.”
Share / Closure
  • Review the completed class Issues Chart together, noting patterns: Political – abuses of monarchy, unfair laws; Economic – poverty, mercantilism; Social – inequality, censorship.
  • Collect exit tickets as a quick reading check to see which problems students understood most clearly.
  • Connect to performance task: “These problems we identified will later help you match Enlightenment thinkers to the issues they criticized – and eventually to revolutions.”
Materials / Resources
  • Three Estates diagram (slide or handout).
  • Station excerpts: Locke, Montesquieu, Adam Smith.
  • Issues Chart (student + large class version).
  • Word bank sheets, sentence frames, exit-ticket slips.
Day 5 SQ2 – Synthesis & Peer Quiz Creation
Day focus: Synthesize Enlightenment critiques of society and turn them into questions through student-created quizzes.
Mini Lesson
  • Quick review of the previous day’s Issues Chart. Students identify one example of a political, social, and economic problem.
  • On the board, sketch a simple cause→effect link between an Enlightenment critique (for example, “taxation without consent”) and a revolutionary action (for example, “Boston Tea Party”).
  • Explain that today they will design quiz questions to check their peers’ understanding of these problems.
Guided Practice
  • In small groups, students pick one category from the Issues Chart (social, economic, or political).
  • Groups create 2–3 multiple-choice or matching questions that connect authors to the problems they described.
  • Teacher circulates and prompts deeper thinking: “Can you phrase that question so it clearly shows what Montesquieu was worried about?”
  • SPED support: provide a quiz template with blank spots for question stems and answer choices to make formatting easier.
Independent Practice
  • Groups exchange quizzes and take another group’s quiz on mini-whiteboards or scrap paper.
  • Students grade the quizzes quickly and note any confusing questions in the margins.
Share / Closure
  • As a class, go over quiz answers, clarifying any common misunderstandings about particular authors or issues.
  • Reflection prompt in notebooks: “Which Enlightenment author’s critique seems most important today, and why?”
  • Check that student notebooks include completed Issues Chart entries; note any students who need reteaching.
Materials / Resources
  • Class Issues Chart from Day 4.
  • Quiz templates (paper or digital).
  • Mini-whiteboards / dry erase markers (optional).
  • Notebooks for reflections.
Day 6 SQ3 – Inspiring Change (Ideas to Action)
Day focus: Analyze how Enlightenment writers inspired people to think differently about their social, political, and economic conditions and practice a short, evidence-based paragraph.
Mini Lesson
  • Pose the question: “How do ideas turn into motivation?”
  • Share a short quote, such as Voltaire’s “Il faut cultiver notre jardin” (“Let us cultivate our garden”) and unpack how it encourages personal and social improvement.
  • Connect this to Kentucky’s civic skills standard HS.WH.I.CC.1 – understanding and discussing how ideas guide actions.
  • Preview vocabulary: inspire, catalyst, reform.
Guided Practice (Jigsaw)
  • Students form three teams: Team 1 – Locke, Team 2 – Montesquieu, Team 3 – Smith (using the same excerpts from SQ2).
  • Each team identifies a line or short passage that sounds like a call for change.
  • Teams paraphrase the quote and write a one-sentence “What they said” explanation (for example, “Locke says all men deserve property as a natural right.”).
  • SPED/ELL support: guiding questions like “What solution does the writer suggest?” and teacher check-ins to support comprehension.
Independent Practice
  • Individually, students write a short paragraph answering SQ3: “According to [writer’s name], how should people think differently about [society/government/etc.]?”
  • They must include at least one quote or specific detail from the text, using a simple Quote → Explanation graphic organizer.
  • Teacher treats this as formative writing and collects paragraphs for quick scoring (focusing on evidence use and basic explanation).
Share / Closure
  • Students pair up and exchange paragraphs, giving feedback: “What idea did your partner identify? Did they use a quote?”
  • A few volunteers read strong paragraphs aloud.
  • Teacher names the skill explicitly: “Today you practiced using specific evidence to show how ideas can inspire change – exactly what our argument rubric and HS.WH.I.UC.2/UC.3 ask you to do.”
Materials / Resources
  • Voltaire quote slide or handout.
  • Locke, Montesquieu, and Smith excerpts (same as Day 4).
  • Quote → Explanation graphic organizer.
  • Paragraph rubric or checklist (simple, 3–4 criteria).
Day 7 SQ4 – Influence to Revolution
Day focus: How did Age of Reason writers and speakers influence people to action and revolution? Students examine Paine and Patrick Henry as catalysts.
Mini Lesson
  • Introduce Paine’s Common Sense and Patrick Henry’s speech as direct catalysts of the American Revolution.
  • Watch a short clip or read lines from "Give me liberty or give me death" to model persuasive oratory.
  • Unpack persuasive techniques (emotive language, analogies, and a clear call to action).
  • Vocabulary focus: patriot, allegiance, revolt.
Guided Practice
  • In pairs, students read Source A: Paine and Source B: Patrick Henry excerpts (from C3).
  • Highlight language that urges action (e.g., "freedom," "British tyranny").
  • Each pair writes a claim statement (for example, "Paine claims the king is a threat to liberty, so colonies should break away.").
  • ELL support: sentence starters such as "Paine says __, which inspires __."
Independent Practice
  • Students draft a claim paragraph answering "What are we inspired to do?" with evidence from Paine or Henry.
  • Encourage direct quotes or paraphrasing with citations to reinforce argument writing.
  • Treat as a formative writing practice to build confidence before the essay.
Share / Closure
  • Volunteers read claims; class discusses which Paine/Henry ideas could inspire revolt.
  • Exit ticket: on a sticky note, students write "One idea from Paine/Henry that inspires action is ___."
  • Collect sticky notes to confirm students recognized links to revolution.
Materials / Resources
  • Excerpts from Common Sense and Patrick Henry’s speech (C3 packet).
  • Short video clip or audio of "Give me liberty or give me death".
  • Highlighters and sentence starter scaffold for claims.
  • Sticky notes for exit tickets.
Day 8 Review & Summative Prep
Day focus: Reconnect SQ1–SQ4 into a clear narrative that shows how Enlightenment ideas moved from critique to action, setting up the summative essay.
Mini Lesson
  • Revisit the compelling question using a simple flow map on the board: ideas → problems → motivating ideas → action → revolution.
  • Summarize prior learning from SQ1–SQ4 to model a cohesive argument.
Guided Practice
  • Students create a timeline or flow-chart linking Enlightenment texts to revolutionary events (paper or Google Slides).
  • Label author, idea, and influence (for example, "Locke (1690) → natural rights → influenced Jefferson").
  • SPED support: provide a partially filled template to complete.
Independent Practice
  • Each student drafts a thesis statement for the summative question: "How did reason lead to revolution?"
  • Teacher circulates to refine wording and connect claims to evidence collected so far.
Share / Closure
  • Selected students share thesis statements for quick feedback.
  • Remind class that the final essay begins tomorrow; confirm everyone has evidence charts ready.
Materials / Resources
  • Board or slide with the flow map arrows.
  • Timeline/flow-chart templates (print and digital).
  • Student evidence charts and notes from previous days.
Day 9 Summative Performance Task – Essay (Part 1)
Day focus: Launch the summative essay on How did Reason lead to Revolution? using the rubric and modeled expectations.
Mini Lesson
  • Restate the task and share the rubric aligned to HS.WH.I.UC.3 and CC.2.
  • Clarify expectations: clear claim, at least three pieces of evidence (Locke, Declaration, Smith, Paine), analysis, and conclusion.
Guided Practice
  • Deconstruct a sample introduction paragraph on the board (thesis, context, lead-in to evidence).
  • Remind students to cite Documents 1–3 explicitly as required in student materials.
  • SPED/ELL support: provide sentence frames and a transition word bank (e.g., "As _ states…", "Therefore…").
Independent Practice
  • Students begin drafting essays in workshop format; teacher conferences with writers about explaining evidence.
  • MLL strategies: bilingual dictionaries and visual cue cards for organization.
  • SPED: extended time and typing/scribe support as needed.
Share / Closure
  • "Writer’s pit stop": partners share thesis and one piece of evidence they will use.
  • No full readings yet—this is a progress check for alignment with the prompt.
Materials / Resources
  • Summative essay rubric and prompt handout.
  • Sample introduction paragraph (projected or printed).
  • Bilingual dictionaries, sentence frames, and transition word bank.
  • Devices or paper for drafting; Document 1–3 packet for citations.
Day 10 Summative Essay (Part 2) & Action Extension
Day focus: Finish the summative essay and begin an optional action-oriented extension connecting Enlightenment ideas to today.
Mini Lesson
  • Review thesis feedback from Day 9 and reiterate linking each body paragraph back to the prompt.
  • Quick reminders on citing the Declaration and other documents accurately.
Guided Practice
  • Students continue drafting while the teacher circulates as a coach, answering targeted questions.
  • Address common writing questions (topic sentences, transitions, citation format).
Independent Practice
  • Full class period for essay completion.
  • Early finishers start an Action Project: a letter to the editor connecting Enlightenment ideas to a current issue (ties to HS.WH.I.CC.3 on proposing solutions).
Share / Closure
  • Brief discussion: "What part of the essay was hardest? Easiest?"
  • Remind students essays and letters will be graded using the rubric reviewed on Day 9.
Materials / Resources
  • Essay rubric and prompts.
  • Letter-to-the-editor prompt and sample stems.
  • Writing devices or notebooks; citation guides.
Day 11 Revision, Peer Review, and Wrap-Up
Day focus: Strengthen essays through peer feedback and targeted revision strategies.
Mini Lesson
  • Teach quick revision moves: add evidence, clarify analysis, check citations and topic sentences.
Guided Practice
  • Students swap essays and use a checklist to give feedback on thesis clarity, evidence use, and explanation.
  • SPED support: simplified checklist with icons/smiley faces (☑ clear thesis, ☑ evidence, ☑ explained).
Independent Practice
  • Students revise essays based on peer feedback.
  • Teacher conferences with students needing major support or IEP accommodations.
Share / Closure
  • Students share one improvement they made (for example, "I added a sentence explaining Locke’s impact.").
  • Final discussion: "Which Age of Reason writer do you admire most, and how do we still see their ideas today?"
Materials / Resources
  • Peer review checklists (standard and simplified versions).
  • Highlighters or colored pens for markup.
  • Access to essay drafts (print or digital).
Day 12 Formative Checkpoints & Reflection
Day focus: Capture evidence of learning through notebook checks, discussion, and reflective writing.
Mini Lesson
  • Explain expectations for the notebook check (charts, quick-writes, reflections) to ensure formative work is complete.
Guided Practice
  • Hold a brief Socratic Seminar on "Were revolutions justified by Enlightenment ideas?" to practice discourse (HS.WH.I.CC.1).
Independent Practice
  • Students write a half-page reflection: "How did reason lead to revolution, based on what we learned? What surprised you?"
  • Collect notebooks for review of formative tasks.
Share / Closure
  • Extension option: mini-project comparing an Enlightenment thinker to a modern figure in a short presentation or poster.
Materials / Resources
  • Student notebooks/journals for collection.
  • Socratic Seminar prompt and discussion norms.
  • Reflection prompt handout or slide; paper for optional extension posters.
Day 13 Summative Presentation & Unit Closure
Day focus: Share action projects, revisit standards, and celebrate completion of the unit.
Mini Lesson
  • Standards recap: connect learning to Kentucky standards (HS.WH.CE.4, HS.WH.CH.6, HS.WH.I.UC.3, HS.WH.I.CC.2, HS.WH.KH.1).
Guided Practice
  • Students present letters or mini-projects; peers offer brief applause and feedback connecting to Enlightenment ideas today.
Independent Practice
  • Teacher returns revised essays and reflections, explaining grading for the summative assessment.
  • Students note personal warm and cool feedback items to guide future writing.
Share / Closure
  • Celebrate with a quick Jeopardy-style quiz game on key names, dates, and ideas.
  • Optional informal celebration (snack, positive shout-outs) to end the unit.
Materials / Resources
  • Standards slide/handout and project presentation setup.
  • Returned essays, feedback sheets, and any letters/projects.
  • Quiz game materials (questions, score sheet) and optional celebration items.

Standards Alignment

This unit ties Enlightenment ideas to revolutions through Kentucky Academic Standards: students analyze cause/effect (HS.WH.CE.4), shifting views on government legitimacy (HS.WH.CH.6), and consistently practice evidence-based writing and argumentation (HS.WH.I.UC.3, HS.WH.I.CC.2). To build discourse skills (HS.WH.I.CC.1), students engage in pair-shares and seminars, and the Kentucky connection (HS.WH.KH.1) surfaces when linking global ideas to the state’s founding principles.
Standard Code Description Where Addressed in Unit
HS.WH.CE.4 Analyze causes and effects of revolutions driven by Enlightenment ideas about rights and government. Introduced with SQ2–SQ4, reinforced in Days 7–10, and assessed in the summative essay and optional action project.
HS.WH.CH.6 Examine how views of government legitimacy and authority changed Highlighted when studying social contracts (Days 2–4) and cited in essays discussing monarchy vs. popular sovereignty.
HS.WH.I.UC.3 & HS.WH.I.CC.2 Use evidence to construct and communicate arguments about historical questions. Practiced through daily claim writing (Days 3–7) and fully applied in the summative essay on Days 9–11.
HS.WH.I.CC.1 Engage in meaningful civic discourse and collaborative discussion. Pair-shares, jigsaws, Socratic Seminar (Day 12), and peer review (Day 11) emphasize respectful dialogue.
HS.WH.KH.1 Connect world ideas to Kentucky history and civic identity. Reflected in Kentucky-specific examples during discussions and the standards recap on Day 13.
