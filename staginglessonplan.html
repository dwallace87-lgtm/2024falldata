Lesson 1 – Staging the Compelling Question & Inalienable Rights

Context

In the previous lesson, 5th period completed a short diagnostic pre assessment on Enlightenment and government. The assessment included 8 multiple choice items on core concepts (natural rights, social contract, consent of the governed, separation of powers), 1 matching cluster where students paired thinkers like Locke, Montesquieu, Rousseau, and Voltaire with simplified descriptions of their ideas, and 1 short constructed response based on a Locke style prompt: “Why might Enlightenment writers argue that people have the right to challenge their government?”

That pre assessment confirmed what I see day to day: most students could recognize some rights language in answer choices, but many struggled to explain those ideas in their own words. I used the item results to note common misconceptions (for example, confusing “rights” with “privileges”) and to make early decisions about small group support for reading and writing. Students who showed very limited understanding on the constructed response are seated closer to the front or near peers who can model strong note taking in this lesson.

5th period is a lively group right after lunch; several students come in chatty but engaged. Reading levels range from about 7th–10th grade. My four IEP students benefit from chunked text, verbal directions, and graphic organizers. The four MLLs are comfortable speaking in English but need explicit vocabulary and the option to jot notes in Spanish. GSSP students usually finish quickly and like being given a leadership role (for example, scribing on the class chart).

This lesson stages the compelling question – “How did Reason lead to Revolution?” – by having students surface their own ideas about rights and then compare those ideas with short excerpts from Locke, the Declaration of Independence, and the Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen. Their notebook quick writes and “Rights Chart” today build on the previous day’s diagnostic data and give me a more qualitative baseline on how comfortably they can talk about inalienable rights in their own words.

Pre assessment item snapshot

Item type Number of items Focus Scoring detail
Multiple choice 8 Core Enlightenment concepts, such as natural rights, social contract, consent of the governed, separation of powers, and basic ideas about the purpose of government. One point per correct answer. These items provided a quick read on prior knowledge and common misconceptions.
Matching (cluster) 1 Matching key thinkers (Locke, Montesquieu, Rousseau, Voltaire) to simplified descriptions of their ideas. Partial credit allowed for partially correct matches. Used primarily to see whether students recognized names and core ideas.
Short constructed response 1 Students responded to a simplified Locke style prompt: “Why might Enlightenment writers argue that people have the right to challenge their government?” Scored on a 0–3 scale for clarity, use of the text, and basic explanation. This writing sample informed early grouping and how much modeling was needed before the essay.

Objectives

  • O1: Define “inalienable rights” in their own words and list at least three examples in their notebooks.
  • O2: Identify and underline key rights language in at least one primary source excerpt (Locke, Declaration of Independence, or Rights of Man) and explain what one phrase means in a sentence.
  • O3: Contribute at least one idea to a class “Rights Chart”, connecting Enlightenment ideas about rights to modern examples (including early Kentucky and U.S. examples) during discussion.

Connections to Kentucky Standards

  • HS.WH.CE.4 – students start tracing causes of revolutions by examining Enlightenment ideas about rights that will later challenge monarchies.
  • HS.WH.CH.6 – lesson introduces how views of government power and legitimacy shift when people believe they have natural rights.
  • HS.WH.I.UC.3 – students use evidence from sources (three excerpts) in their charts and written responses.
  • HS.WH.KH.1 – brief connection from Enlightenment rights language to Kentucky’s early constitutional protections, such as religious freedom.

Procedures (Workshop Structure)

Phase Time Teacher actions Student actions Differentiation / notes
Launch 5 min Greet students at the door; Do Now on the board: “When you hear the word right, what comes to mind?” Preview that the class is starting a new unit on Enlightenment and Revolution. Jot 2–3 ideas about rights in notebooks and share with a shoulder partner. A few students share out with the whole class. Sentence starter on board: “A right is when people are allowed to…”. Cue a quieter student ahead of time so the first share feels safe.
Mini lesson 12–15 min Project the Salon of Madame Geoffrin painting and ask: “What do you notice? What might they be debating?” Guide observations toward ideas, books, arguments, government. Introduce the term inalienable rights and connect to student examples (life, liberty, property, free speech). Write the compelling question on the board and underline it: “How did Reason lead to Revolution?”. Explain that today is about understanding the rights part of that question. Participate in “notice and wonder” about the painting. Offer examples of rights they think everyone should have. Copy the compelling question into notebooks and star it. Vocabulary is posted with icons. Allow think–pair–share before whole group volunteering. Intentionally validate all attempts to build trust early in the year.
Guided practice 15–18 min Distribute the three excerpts and the Rights Chart organizer. Model one example on the document camera: read a sentence aloud, underline “life, liberty, pursuit of happiness,” and think aloud about what that means. Give each table a different starting document and circulate, prompting: “Underline any phrase that sounds like a right people have just because they are human.” Read adapted excerpts in partners or trios. Underline phrases that describe rights and record them in the organizer under “Examples of inalienable rights.” Check understanding at their table using quick oral explanations such as “This phrase means…”. SPED: partially filled chart and highlighted sentences in the text. MLL: bilingual glossary and permission to annotate in Spanish. GSSP: challenged to find two less obvious rights (for example, security or resistance to oppression) to share later.
Independent practice 10–12 min Project and read aloud the prompt: “According to these documents, what are examples of inalienable rights?”. Circulate and confer with students who have incomplete charts or vague answers. In notebooks, students write a short response naming at least three rights they saw in the documents and add one genuine question they still have about rights (for example, “Is education an inalienable right?”). Serves as a baseline formative check for writing about rights. Allow extended time and chunk the prompt for students with writing accommodations. Offer optional sentence stems for those who need extra structure.
Share & closure 5–8 min Invite several students, including at least one MLL and one IEP student who felt successful, to share one right from their notebook. Record these on a large class Inalienable Rights Chart that stays posted. Close by connecting to Kentucky: early Kentucky documents also protected rights such as religious freedom, foreshadowing how these Enlightenment ideas spread. Add any new rights from the class chart to their own list. Respond to a final prompt: “Circle the right on your list that feels most important to you and be ready to explain why next class.” Quick “fist to five” confidence check: “How confident are you that you could explain what an inalienable right is?” This gives a fast read on whole class comfort before SQ1 tomorrow.

Assessment Plan

Objective Assessment method Type What I’m looking for / how I’ll use it
O1 – Define and list inalienable rights Opening “rights” Do Now plus notebook response to: “What rights do you think everyone is born with?” Pre assessment Skim several during work time, then more after class, to establish a baseline of how many rights students can name without support. Record quick tallies (0–1 rights, 2–3 rights, 4+ rights) in my data tracker.
O2 – Identify rights language in texts Rights Chart organizer and underlined phrases in the excerpts; informal questioning while circulating. Formative Glance at charts from at least 6–8 students across ability levels. If many have only “life and liberty,” I know I need to reteach lesser known rights such as security or resistance to oppression before moving on.
O3 – Contribute to class Rights Chart and connect to context Whole class share and construction of the posted Rights Chart; brief oral responses linking rights to modern or Kentucky examples. Formative Track who volunteers and who I invite in future discussions. The posted chart becomes a reference tool for later lessons and for the summative essay about how Reason leads to Revolution.

Note: No summative assessment happens in this single lesson. Content knowledge baseline comes from the previous day’s diagnostic pre assessment (8 multiple choice items, 1 matching cluster, 1 constructed response on Locke). Today’s quick writes and Rights Charts provide additional qualitative baseline data on how students describe inalienable rights in their own words before we move deeper into the inquiry.
