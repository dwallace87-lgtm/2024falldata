Lesson 1 – Staging the Compelling Question & Inalienable Rights

Context

In the previous lesson, 5th period completed a short diagnostic pre-assessment on Enlightenment and government. The assessment had 8 multiple-choice items on core concepts (natural rights, social contract, consent of the governed, separation of powers), 1 matching cluster where students paired thinkers like Locke, Montesquieu, Rousseau, and Voltaire with simplified descriptions of their ideas, and 1 short constructed response based on a Locke-style prompt: “Why might Enlightenment writers argue that people have the right to challenge their government?”

That pre-assessment confirmed what I see day to day: most students could recognize some rights language in answer choices, but many struggled to explain those ideas in their own words. I used the item results to note common misconceptions (for example, confusing “rights” with “privileges”) and to make early decisions about small-group support for reading and writing. Students who showed very limited understanding on the constructed response are seated closer to the front or near peers who can model strong note-taking in this lesson.

5th period is a lively group right after lunch; several students come in chatty but engaged. Reading levels range from about 7th–10th grade. My four IEP students benefit from chunked text, verbal directions, and graphic organizers. The four MLLs are comfortable speaking in English but need explicit vocabulary and the option to jot notes in Spanish. GSSP students usually finish quickly and like being given a leadership role (for example, scribing on the class chart).

This lesson stages the compelling question – “How did Reason lead to Revolution?” – by having students surface their own ideas about rights and then compare those ideas with short excerpts from Locke, the Declaration of Independence, and the Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen. Their notebook quick-writes and “Rights Chart” today build on the previous day’s diagnostic data and give me a more qualitative baseline on how comfortably they can talk about inalienable rights in their own words.

Objectives

By the end of this 45-minute lesson, students will be able to:

  • O1: Define “inalienable rights” in their own words and list at least three examples in their notebooks.
  • O2: Identify and underline key rights language in at least one primary-source excerpt (Locke, Declaration of Independence, or Rights of Man) and explain what one phrase means in a sentence.
  • O3: Contribute at least one idea to a class “Rights Chart”, connecting Enlightenment ideas about rights to modern examples (including early Kentucky and U.S. examples) during discussion.

Connections to Kentucky Standards

  • HS.WH.CE.4 – Students start tracing causes of revolutions by examining Enlightenment ideas about rights that will later challenge monarchies.
  • HS.WH.CH.6 – Lesson introduces how views of government power and legitimacy shift when people believe they have natural rights.
  • HS.WH.I.UC.3 – Students use evidence from sources (three excerpts) in their charts and written responses.
  • HS.WH.KH.1 – I briefly connect Enlightenment rights language to Kentucky’s early constitutional protections, such as religious freedom, so students see local relevance.

Procedures

I use a workshop structure: Launch / Mini-Lesson / Guided Practice / Independent Practice / Share and Closure. Times are approximate for a 45-minute period.

Phase Time Teacher Actions Student Actions Differentiation / Notes
Launch 5 min Greet students at the door; quick Do Now on the board: “When you hear the word right, what comes to mind?” Once seated, preview that we are starting a new unit on Enlightenment and Revolution. Jot 2–3 ideas about “rights” in notebooks and share with a shoulder partner. A few students share out whole-group. Sentence starter on board: “A right is when people are allowed to…”. I cue a quieter student ahead of time so the first share feels safe.
Mini-Lesson 12–15 min
  • Project the Salon of Madame Geoffrin painting and ask, “What do you notice? What might they be debating?”
  • Guide observations toward ideas, books, arguments, government.
  • Introduce the term inalienable rights and connect to student examples (life, liberty, property, free speech).
  • Write the compelling question on the board, underlined: “How did Reason lead to Revolution?” Explain that today is about understanding the “rights” piece of that question.
  • Participate in “notice and wonder” about the painting.
  • Offer examples of rights they think everyone should have.
  • Copy the compelling question into notebooks and star it.
 Vocabulary is posted with icons. I allow think-pair-share before whole-group volunteering. I intentionally validate all attempts to build trust this early in the year.
Guided Practice 15–18 min
  • Distribute the three excerpts and the Rights Chart organizer.
  • Model one example on the document camera: read one sentence aloud, underline “life, liberty, pursuit of happiness,” and think aloud about what that means.
  • Give each table a different starting document and circulate, prompting: “Underline any phrase that sounds like a right people have just because they are human.”
  • Read adapted excerpts in partners or trios.
  • Underline phrases that describe rights and jot them in the organizer under “Examples of inalienable rights.”
  • Check understanding at their table using quick oral explanations: “This phrase means…”.
 SPED: partially filled chart and highlighted sentences in the text. MLL: use bilingual glossary and annotate in Spanish if needed. GSSP: find two less obvious rights (security, resistance to oppression) to share later.
Independent Practice 10–12 min Prompt on board (projected and read aloud): “According to these documents, what are examples of inalienable rights?” Circulate and confer with students who have incomplete charts or vague answers. In notebooks, students:
  • Write a short response naming at least three rights they saw in the documents.
  • Add one genuine question they still have about rights (for example, “Is education an inalienable right?”).
 Serves as a baseline pre-assessment for writing about rights. I note students who cannot name any specific rights so I can group them strategically tomorrow.
Share and Closure 5–8 min Invite several students, including at least one MLL and one IEP student who felt successful, to share one right from their notebook. Record these on a large Class “Inalienable Rights” Chart that stays posted. Close by connecting to Kentucky: early Kentucky documents also protected rights like religious freedom, foreshadowing how these Enlightenment ideas spread. Add any new rights from the class chart to their own list. Respond to a final prompt: “Circle the right on your list that feels most important to you and be ready to explain why next class.” Quick “fist-to-five” confidence check: “How confident are you that you could explain what an inalienable right is?” This gives a fast read on whole-class comfort before SQ1 tomorrow.

Assessment Plan

Assessments below are used within this single lesson and feed into the larger unit data set described in my outcomes documents.

Objective Assessment Method Type What I’m Looking For / How I’ll Use It
O1 – Define and list inalienable rights Opening “rights” Do Now plus notebook response to: “What rights do you think everyone is born with?” Pre-assessment I skim several during work time, then more after class, to establish a baseline of how many rights students can name without support. I record quick tallies (0–1 rights, 2–3 rights, 4+ rights) in my data tracker.
O2 – Identify rights language in texts Rights Chart organizer and underlined phrases in the excerpts; informal questioning while circulating. Formative I glance at charts from at least 6–8 students across ability levels. If many have only “life and liberty,” I know I need to reteach lesser-known rights such as security or resistance to oppression before moving on.
O3 – Contribute to class Rights Chart and connect to context Whole-class share and construction of the posted Rights Chart; brief oral responses linking rights to modern or Kentucky examples. Formative I track who volunteers and who I invite in future discussions. The posted chart becomes a reference tool for later lessons and for the summative essay about how Reason leads to Revolution.

*No summative assessment happens in this single lesson. Content knowledge baseline comes from the previous day’s diagnostic pre-assessment (8 multiple choice items, 1 matching cluster, 1 constructed response on Locke). Today’s quick-writes and Rights Charts provide additional qualitative baseline data on how students describe inalienable rights in their own words before we move deeper into the inquiry.
