Lesson 1 – Staging the Compelling Question & Inalienable Rights

Context

This is the first day of our Enlightenment & Revolution inquiry for 5th period. Students have just finished a short review mini-unit on absolute monarchy and the causes of the American Revolution, so they have some language for “kings, colonies, and protests,” but most have not yet used terms like inalienable rights or social contract in their own writing.

5th period is a lively group right after lunch; several students come in chatty but engaged. Reading levels range from about 7th–10th grade. My four IEP students benefit from chunked text, verbal directions, and graphic organizers. The four MLLs are comfortable speaking in English but need explicit vocabulary and the option to jot notes in Spanish. GSSP students usually finish quickly and like being given a leadership role (for example, scribing on the class chart).

This lesson stages the compelling question – “How did Reason lead to Revolution?” – by having students surface their own ideas about rights, then compare those ideas with excerpts from Locke, the Declaration of Independence, and the Declaration of the Rights of Man. Their notebook quick-writes and “Rights Chart” today will become the baseline pre-assessment data for the rest of the unit.

Objectives

By the end of this 55-minute lesson, students will be able to:

  • O1: Define “inalienable rights” in their own words and list at least three examples in their notebooks.
  • O2: Identify and underline key rights language in at least one primary-source excerpt (Locke, Declaration of Independence, or Rights of Man) and explain what one phrase means in a sentence.
  • O3: Contribute at least one idea to a class “Rights Chart”, connecting Enlightenment ideas about rights to modern examples (including early Kentucky / U.S. examples) during discussion.

Connections to Kentucky Standards

  • HS.WH.CE.4 – Students begin tracing causes of revolutions by examining Enlightenment ideas about rights that will later challenge monarchies.
  • HS.WH.CH.6 – Lesson introduces how views of government power & legitimacy shift when people believe they have natural rights.
  • HS.WH.I.UC.3 – Students use evidence from sources (three excerpts) in their charts and written responses.
  • HS.WH.KH.1 – I briefly connect Enlightenment rights language to Kentucky’s early constitutional protections (e.g., freedom of religion).

Resources, Media, & Technology

Materials

  • Projected image of Madame Geoffrin’s salon (public-domain painting).
  • Adapted excerpts packet: Locke’s Two Treatises, U.S. Declaration of Independence, and French Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen.
  • Student notebooks / journals.
  • Chart paper or whiteboard space for the class “Rights Chart.”
  • “Rights Chart” individual graphic organizer (standard and modified versions).

Technology & Supports

  • LCD projector / speakers for image and (optional) short video clip.
  • Google Classroom post with PDFs of the excerpts and a digital organizer for absent students.
  • Bilingual (English–Spanish) vocabulary slide and printable glossary for key terms like inalienable, liberty, charter, liberté.
  • Permission for MLL students to use translation apps on Chromebooks when stuck on a word.
  • Document camera for modeling how to underline and annotate the texts.

Procedures

I use a workshop structure: Launch / Mini-Lesson / Guided Practice / Independent Practice / Share & Closure. Times are approximate and based on a 55-minute period.

Phase Time Teacher Actions Student Actions Differentiation / Notes
Launch 5 min Greet students at the door; quick “Do Now” on the board: “When you hear the word right, what comes to mind?” Once seated, briefly preview that we are starting a new unit on Enlightenment & Revolution. Students jot 2–3 ideas about “rights” in their notebooks and share with a shoulder partner. A few students share out whole-group. I cue quieter students beforehand to be ready to share. Provide sentence starter on board: “A right is when people are allowed to…”.
Mini-Lesson 12–15 min
  • Project the Salon of Madame Geoffrin painting and ask, “What do you notice? What might they be debating?”
  • Guide observations toward ideas, books, arguments, and government.
  • Introduce the term inalienable rights and connect to student examples (life, liberty, property, free speech).
  • Write the compelling question on the board, underlined: “How did Reason lead to Revolution?” Explain that today is about understanding the “rights” piece of that question.
  • Participate in a brief “notice & wonder” about the painting.
  • Offer examples of rights they think everyone should have.
  • Copy the compelling question into notebooks and star it.
 Vocabulary is posted with icons. I cold-call a mix of students but allow think-pair-share before answering. I intentionally validate all attempts to build classroom trust, especially this early in the year.
Guided Practice 15–18 min
  • Distribute the three excerpts (Locke, U.S. Declaration, Rights of Man) and the Rights Chart organizer.
  • Model one example on the document camera: read one sentence aloud, then underline “life, liberty, pursuit of happiness” and think aloud about what that means.
  • Give each table a different starting document and circulate, prompting: “Underline any phrase that sounds like a right people have just because they are human.”
  • Read short adapted excerpts in partners or trios.
  • Underline phrases that describe rights and jot them in the organizer under “Examples of inalienable rights.”
  • Check understanding at their table using quick oral explanations (“This phrase means…”).
 SPED: receive a partially filled chart and highlighted sentences in the text. MLL: use bilingual glossary; may annotate in Spanish first, then English. GSSP: challenged to find two less obvious rights (e.g., security, resistance to oppression) and be ready to share.
Independent Practice 10–12 min Prompt on board (projected and read aloud): “According to these documents, what are examples of inalienable rights?” Circulate, conferring briefly with students who have incomplete charts or vague answers. In their notebooks, students:
  • Write a short response naming at least three rights they saw in the documents.
  • Add one genuine question they still have about rights (for example, “Is education an inalienable right?”).
 This serves as a baseline pre-assessment. I quietly jot notes about students who struggle to name any rights so I can group them strategically tomorrow.
Share & Closure 5–8 min Invite several students (including at least one MLL and one IEP student who felt successful) to share one right from their notebook. Record these on a large Class “Inalienable Rights” Chart that will stay posted. Close by connecting to Kentucky: mention that early Kentucky documents also protected rights like religious freedom, foreshadowing how these Enlightenment ideas spread. Students listen, add any new rights to their own list if they missed them, and respond to a final prompt: “Circle the right on your list that feels most important to you and be ready to explain why next class.” Quick “fist-to-five” check: “How confident are you that you could explain what an inalienable right is?” This gives me a fast read on whole-class comfort level before moving into SQ1 tomorrow.

Assessment Plan

The assessments below are used within this single lesson and feed into the larger unit data set described in my outcomes documents.

Objective Assessment Method Type What I’m Looking For / How I’ll Use It
O1 – Define & list inalienable rights Opening “rights” Do-Now plus notebook response to: “What rights do you think everyone is born with?” Pre-assessment I skim several during work time, then more after class, to establish a baseline of how many rights students can name without support. I record quick tallies (0–1 rights, 2–3 rights, 4+ rights) for my data tracker.
O2 – Identify rights language in texts Rights Chart organizer and underlined phrases in each excerpt; informal questioning while circulating. Formative I glance at charts from at least 6–8 students across ability levels. If many have only “life & liberty,” I know I need to re-teach the lesser-known rights (e.g., security, resistance to oppression) before moving on.
O3 – Contribute to class Rights Chart & connect to context Whole-class share-out and construction of the posted Rights Chart; brief oral responses linking rights to modern life. Formative I track who volunteers and who I need to invite in future discussions. The posted chart becomes a reference tool for later lessons and for the summative essay (“Reason → Revolution”).

*No summative assessment occurs in this single lesson; it functions as the staged launch and diagnostic writing piece for the broader Enlightenment & Revolution argument essay later in the unit.
