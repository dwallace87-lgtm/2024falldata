10th Grade World History, Inquiry Design Model
Class Profile
5th Period World History – 19 students total: 4 students with IEPs, 3 multilingual learners, 1 GSSP student, mix of readers who are confident and students still building skills.
Unit Title
Enlightenment and Revolutions Inquiry
Lesson Number and Title
Lesson 1 – Introduction to the Age of Reason and inalienable rights (Staging the compelling question).

Context

This lesson opens our Enlightenment and Revolutions unit for 5th period at Franklin County High School. It builds directly from yesterday's diagnostic pre assessment on Enlightenment ideas, which gave me a baseline for what students already understand and where they are confused. In that pre assessment, most students recognized the phrase natural rights but were less confident with terms like social contract and consent of the governed. Several students with IEPs and multilingual learners left the short response blank or wrote very brief answers.

Today I want to hook students with a visual, build shared language around inalienable rights, and give every student an accessible way to join the conversation. The lesson uses the workshop model that my classes know well: mini lesson, guided practice, independent work, and share/closure, with supports built in for SPED and MLL students.

Pre assessment snapshot (previous lesson)

Item type Number of items Focus Scoring detail
Multiple choice 8 Core Enlightenment concepts such as natural rights, social contract, consent of the governed, separation of powers, and basic ideas about the purpose of government. One point per correct answer. Gave a quick read on prior knowledge and common misconceptions.
Matching (cluster) 1 set Matching key thinkers (Locke, Montesquieu, Rousseau, Voltaire) to simplified descriptions of their ideas. Partial credit allowed for partially correct matches. Used mainly to see whether students recognized names and core ideas.
Short constructed response 1 Prompt: in a simplified Locke style, students answered "Why might Enlightenment writers argue that people have the right to challenge their government?" Scored on a 0–3 scale for clarity, use of the text, and basic explanation. Writing samples informed groupings and how much modeling would be needed before the full essay.

Objectives

  • Students will be able to identify and define inalienable rights and give at least three examples in writing.
  • Students will be able to locate rights language in short adapted excerpts from Locke, the Declaration of Independence, and the Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen.
  • Students will be able to pose at least one question they still have about rights, which will guide future lessons and small group support.

Connections to Kentucky Standards

  • HS.WH.CE.4 – causes and effects of political revolutions (students explore Enlightenment ideas about rights that will later fuel revolutions).
  • HS.WH.CH.6 – changing views of government and legitimacy (students consider how ideas like natural rights challenge absolute monarchy).
  • HS.WH.KH.1 – connections to Kentucky history (brief reference to religious freedom in Kentucky's 1792 constitution as an Enlightenment legacy).

Resources, Media, and Technology

  • Projected image of an 18th century salon (Madame Geoffrin's salon, 1755).
  • Adapted excerpts from Locke's Two Treatises, the Declaration of Independence, and the Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen.
  • Rights Chart organizer (standard and modified versions for SPED/MLL students).
  • Student notebooks, chart paper, markers, highlighters.
  • Optional: document camera or projector for whole class think aloud annotation.

Procedures (Workshop Model)

Phase Teacher actions Student actions Differentiation / notes
Hook and Mini Lesson Project the Enlightenment salon image and prompt discussion: "What might people in this scene be talking about?" Connect their ideas to the Age of Reason and introduce the phrase inalienable rights. Model examples on the board (life, liberty, property, free speech, education) and explain how these ideas will later challenge kings and empires. Share observations about the image, respond to prompting questions, and complete a quick write in notebooks: "What rights do you think everyone is born with? Give examples." Cold call intentionally but support anxious speakers. Allow think time and sentence starters for students who need them. This portion activates background knowledge from the pre assessment without restating all items.
Guided Practice Distribute short adapted excerpts from the three documents. Lead a think aloud with one paragraph, modeling how to underline rights language. Then circulate as groups read and complete a shared Rights Chart. In groups, read assigned excerpts, highlight phrases about rights, and record rights from each document in the Rights Chart. Ask clarifying questions as needed. Provide a bilingual glossary for MLL students and allow translation apps. SPED students receive a partially completed Rights Chart so they can focus on locating and recording key words instead of copying long text.
Independent Practice Restate the prompt and remind students to use their charts and notes. Quietly scan a sample of notebooks while students work, noting who may need reteaching or small group support next class. Independently answer in notebooks: "According to these documents, what are examples of inalienable rights?" List at least three rights and write one question they still have about rights. Allow extended time and chunking of the prompt for students with writing accommodations. Offer optional sentence stems like "One inalienable right is..." for students who need structure.
Share and Closure Invite several students to share their lists. Build a class anchor chart of inalienable rights and leave it posted. Close by noting that similar rights appear in early Kentucky documents, such as freedom of religion in the 1792 constitution. Share examples aloud, help add to the class chart, and listen for similarities across classmates' ideas. Use this share time to normalize questions about rights. Acknowledge insightful questions that can feed future lessons on revolutions.

Assessment Plan

Objective Assessment method Evidence of learning When collected
Define inalienable rights and list at least three examples. Quick write and notebook response. Student responses include a workable definition in their own words and at least three appropriate examples of rights. During independent practice; teacher scans notebooks the same day.
Locate rights language in the three foundational documents. Rights Chart organizer (group work) with informal teacher observation. Completed charts show that groups have correctly pulled out phrases such as "life, liberty, property" and "men are born and remain free and equal in rights." During guided practice; charts checked at end of class or early next period.
Articulate at least one question or uncertainty about rights. Question portion of notebook response and whole class share. Students write a genuine question about rights. I note patterns (for example, many students wondering whether education counts as an inalienable right) to inform future mini lessons and small group supports. Collected informally at closure; notes used in planning Day 2 and later lessons.
