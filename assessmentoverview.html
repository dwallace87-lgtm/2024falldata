Enlightenment & Revolution – Assessment Overview

Franklin County High School • 10th Grade World History • 2024–2025 School Year

Teacher: Donald Wallace Total students: 118 Inquiry design model (C3)

Context and unit snapshot

Course
10th Grade World History at Franklin County High School in Frankfort, Kentucky.
Team context
I teach World History as part of a small team. During the 2024 to 2025 school year I ran this inquiry in parallel with my colleague Stephanie Morrell, who taught her own World History sections. We planned around the same Kentucky Academic Standards and general pacing, but this performance task and data set reflect only my 118 students across five sections.
Unit focus
An inquiry on the Enlightenment and political revolutions centered on the compelling question "How did Reason lead to Revolution?"
Timeline
Early in the first semester, over about three weeks. For most students this was their first formal historical argument essay of the year.
Standards alignment
Aligned to the Kentucky Academic Standards for Social Studies (High School World History) in areas that address the Enlightenment, changing views of government and legitimacy, political revolutions, and inquiry based argument writing. The performance task asked students to interpret sources, trace cause and effect, and construct an evidence based written argument.

I used an Inquiry Design Model structure for this work. Students engaged with a compelling question, several supporting questions, primary and secondary sources, and a final argument performance task. The goal was to introduce them to the kind of inquiry and writing Kentucky expects in high school World History, while still giving them enough scaffolding to feel successful early in the year.

Students and classes included

This assessment overview covers 118 students across five World History sections:

  • Period 1 - 24 students
  • Period 2 - 23 students
  • Period 3 - 26 students
  • Period 5 - 26 students
  • Period 7 - 19 students (a smaller class with a mix of students who needed additional support and a few strong peer models)

These rosters included multilingual learners, students with IEPs, and students with significant attendance challenges. I collaborated with Hannah Bennett, one of our special education teachers, who works with several of these students during her class period. Hannah and I stayed in touch about organizers, vocabulary supports, and expectations so that students felt the connection between her class and mine rather than experiencing two completely separate worlds.

Learning goals

Big idea
I wanted students to move beyond memorizing names and dates and start to explain how ideas travel, how people challenge power, and how revolutions connect to arguments about rights and government.

Content goals

  • Describe key Enlightenment ideas such as natural rights, social contract, separation of powers, and religious tolerance in accessible language.
  • Connect Enlightenment ideas to political revolutions, especially the American and French Revolutions, by explaining how new ideas about rights and government challenged older systems.
  • Recognize that Enlightenment and revolution look different in different regions, and that the outcomes of revolutions are not always simple or positive.

Disciplinary and inquiry goals

  • Work with short, adapted primary and secondary sources to pull out main ideas and relevant evidence.
  • Explain cause and effect in writing, not just list events. For example, explain how natural rights theory can lead people to question a monarch.
  • Use an argument structure that includes a clear claim, supporting reasons, and specific evidence from sources.

Literacy and writing goals

  • Plan and write a multi paragraph argument essay that answers the compelling question and stays focused on that question.
  • Use at least two sources to support the argument and explain the evidence rather than just dropping in quotes.
  • Organize writing with basic transitions and maintain readable sentence structure, even if students are still building more advanced style.

Assessment plan and evidence sources

Diagnostic pre assessment

Before we launched the full inquiry, students completed a short pre assessment on Enlightenment and government to give me a baseline. The pre assessment included:

  • Eight multiple choice questions on core ideas like natural rights, social contract, and the purpose of government.
  • One matching cluster that asked students to connect thinkers such as Locke, Montesquieu, Rousseau, and Voltaire to simplified descriptions of their ideas.
  • One short constructed response that asked: In your own words, why might Enlightenment writers argue that people have the right to challenge their government?

Students had access to a short adapted passage or textbook style explanation, but they did not receive sentence starters for this pre assessment. I wanted a clear picture of how they explained ideas without heavy scaffolds yet. Those scores became the baseline in the data file.

Formative tasks during the inquiry

Throughout the unit, students completed several formative tasks that built toward the final essay:

  • A jigsaw reading on major Enlightenment thinkers, where each group read a short adapted text and then taught their classmates the main idea in simple language.
  • Source analysis stations that asked students to highlight evidence and answer focused questions about how each source viewed government and power.
  • A planning organizer that guided students to write a claim, list reasons, and match each reason to at least one source.
  • A practice body paragraph, written together in class, that modeled how to connect an Enlightenment idea to a specific change during a revolution.

These formative checkpoints were scored for completion and basic quality. They helped me decide where to slow down or re model before the summative essay.

Summative performance task

The culminating task for this inquiry was a written argument essay in response to the question "How did Reason lead to Revolution?" Students wrote in class using their notes and organizers. They were expected to:

  • State a clear claim that directly answers the question.
  • Use at least two sources from the unit, cited in simple, student friendly ways.
  • Explain how Enlightenment ideas influenced political revolutions, not just list facts.
  • Organize their writing into an introduction, body paragraphs, and a brief conclusion.

I scored essays with a four part rubric that focused on claim and focus, use of evidence, historical reasoning, and writing conventions. This is the same rubric I planned to use again later in the course so I could track growth over time.

Support, differentiation, and collaboration

Support for multilingual learners and students with IEPs

For multilingual learners and students with IEPs, I created adjusted versions of the graphic organizers with sentence stems and small word banks. I also front loaded key vocabulary in short warm ups so students could hear and use the language before they saw it in the texts. During the writing process, I pulled small groups to rehearse ideas out loud before they tried to put everything on paper.

I coordinated these supports with Hannah Bennett, who works with several of the same students in a different class period. We shared organizers and talked about which sentence stems and routines were working, so students felt some continuity instead of starting from scratch every time they shifted from her room to mine.

Support for attendance challenges

I also planned short catch up pathways for students with significant absences. For each key formative task, I created a shorter version that a student could complete during advisory or at home. This allowed them to at least experience the core reading and writing moves before they attempted the essay. Those catch up tasks are referenced in my data and analysis documents, since attendance shows up clearly in the outcomes.
